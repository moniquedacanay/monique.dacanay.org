Model Description

This project models the sea life of sea turtles and jellyfish. Two versions of the project were created; the first uses AgentCubes, and the second uses Vensim.

The Story

If a jellyfish (pink square) is one of the nearest-neighbors of a sea turtle (green square), it will test the wave direction (a simulation property, WAVE_DIRECTION -- 0 means north, 1 means south, 2 means east, 3 means west). If the jellyfish is downward from the sea turtle, there is some percent chance (simulation property, HIGH_PCT_CHANCE) that the jellyfish will be eaten by the sea turtle. If the jellyfish is NOT downward from the sea turtle, there is a different percent chance (simulation property, LOW_PCT_CHANCE) that the jellyfish will still be eaten by the sea turtle. Also, plastic (gray square) can appear in the world caused by human littering (humans not actually shown in the model). If the sea turtle eats or comes in contact with the plastic, then there is some percent chance, (simulation property, DEATH_PCT_CHANCE) that the sea turtle will die or disappear from the ocean. If a jellyfish is next to another jellyfish, there is some percent chance, (simulation property, REPRODUCTION_PCT_CHANCE), that another jellyfish will be born, or appear in the ocean. If a sea turtle is next to another sea turtle, there is some percent chance, (simulation property, REPRODUCTION_PCT_CHANCE), that another sea turtle will be born, or appear in the ocean. Plastic appears randomly in the ocean. Plastic moves randomly in the ocean. Jellyfish moves randomly in the ocean. Sea turtles move towards jellyfish and plastic, when hungry. A counter (purple square) counts the number of sea turtles, jellyfish, and plastic there is in the ocean, and graphs the population sizes. To change any of the simulation properties, click the gear button at the top left part of the screen. Then, in the menu that appears, click show simulation properties. In the simulation dialog box, you can change any values and you can see what are the properties, in which affect the model.

Observed Behaviors

In the models, jellyfish and sea turtles swim in the ocean. When hungry, sea turtles try to look for jellyfish, but sometimes, accidentally eat plastic instead. If they do eat the plastic or they come in contact with it, there is a percent chance that they will die. Also, jellyfish can give birth to more jellyfish, when they come in contact with another jellyfish. In the same way, sea turtles can give birth to more sea turtles, when they interact with other sea turtles. These models show how both populations depend on each other, and affect each other in many ways. In the graph, you can see that one population would rise, while the other would fall, and the opposite would also later occur. The other population would start to rise, while the first population would fall. For example, if the sea turtle population rose, then this would cause the jellyfish population to decrease. Eventually, the sea turtle population would compete with each other for food (jellyfish), and their population would decrease, because of natural selection. When the sea turtle population decreased, the jellyfish population would increase again, because of lack of predators. This would cycle, with both populations going up and down. In the models, the jellyfish and plastic bags, would move randomly in the world, while the sea turtles would hunt for them (jellyfish and plastic bags). Like in the real world, sea turtles may mistakenly eat a plastic bag, thinking it was jellyfish, since they both look very similar to each other. In addition, in this model, plastic appears randomly in the ocean (caused by human littering).

Nearest-Neighbors

I ensured that only the nearest neighbors get eaten, by broadcasting a jellyfish check and change. This makes it so that only the nearest neighbors get eaten, because in one time step, instead of seeing that the sea turtles are beside jellyfish and eating them, or making them disappear, it sets the food of the jellyfish beside the sea turtle to one, then after, in the broadcast jellyfish change, it will test if the food of the jellyfish is set to one. If it is, then the jellyfish will then get eaten by the sea turtle. This prevents too many jellyfish, or in other words, only the nearest neighbors to get eaten by sea turtles. This is very important in making only those jellyfish right beside the sea turtle(s) to get eaten.

Conclusions

I learned that two or more populations can greatly affect each other. Like stated before, if the sea turtle population rose, then this would cause the jellyfish population to decrease. Later, competition between many sea turtles will result in the decrease of sea turtles, and the increase of jellyfish. This would cycle continuously, having one population rise, and the other fall. I also learned that many different factors can result in the deaths of different organisms. For example, the plastic bags in these models hurt the sea turtle populations and made them decrease. In research, other factors like starvation, old age, body injury, suffocation, and other things can result in the deaths of organisms.
